Date: 2026-06-14
System: SELENE (Moon, south pole)
Baseline: SJSU Phase 1b, 8.7 MW solar
Accuracy: Order-of-magnitude (±50%)

Important caveat

These are first-principles engineering estimates, not a formal cost study. Uncertainty band is roughly 0.5× to 2× on any individual line item, and 0.6× to 1.8× on the total. 2. Physics Baseline (all bore sizes)

The Phase 1b system is power-limited, not bore-limited when launching bulk mass at escape speed. Annual throughput is set by solar power, not by how wide the tube is. Bore diameter determines shot mass and market reach, not tonnes-per-year for bulk.

-- Wall-plug energy per kg at escape speed -- E = v² / (2 × η) = 2,385² / (2 × 3.6×10⁶ × 0.84) = 0.941 kWh/kg -- Annual energy per installed MW (south-pole site) -- E_yr = 8,760 h × 0.90 illumination = 7,884 MWh/MW/yr -- Throughput rule -- T = E_yr / E_shot = 7,884,000 kWh ÷ 0.941 kWh/kg = 8,378 t/MW/yr at escape speed = 2,706 t/MW/yr at 4.2 km/s (Phase 3) -- SJSU Phase 1b: 8.7 MW, 25.4 kg buckets, 10 s cadence -- shots/yr = 7,884 h × 3,600 s/h × 0.90 / 10 s = 2.84 M shots/yr mass/yr = 2.84 × 10⁶ × 25.4 kg = ~72,000 t/yr (all bore sizes equal at bulk)

Key takeaway

The three bore options differ by only $200-300M in total build cost. The REBCO premium for going from Ø1 m to Ø3 m is $152M (mid), roughly 5% of total project cost. Bore choice barely moves the budget needle. What changes is what markets you can serve.

5. Unit Economics: Cost per Kilogram Launched

At 72,000 t/yr and a 10-year operating life, the system launches 720 million kg of bulk material. The amortized cost per kg is the metric that matters for competitiveness against rockets.

Comparison: Starship lunar delivery (2030s optimistic): $200-500/kg. Falcon 9 GTO: ~$3,000/kg. Apollo-era lunar surface: ~$1,000,000/kg (inflation adjusted).
The number that matters

At $3-8/kg amortized, SELENE undercuts Starship by 25x to 100x on bulk mass delivery to cislunar orbit. This advantage is structural, not incremental. The bore size changes this cost by less than 12% across all scenarios.

Put differently: the $152M premium to upgrade from Ø1 m to Ø3 m is recovered in less than three days of full-capacity operation at even $10/kg market pricing.

6. What Bore Size Actually Changes

Since throughput (tonnes/year) and unit cost ($/kg) are nearly identical across bore sizes, the real question is: what cargo categories does each bore unlock?

The pod constraint

All bore sizes are limited to 2 m pod length in this baseline. A payload that is 3 m long will not fit in any of these configurations regardless of bore diameter. Track length governs exit speed; pod dimensions govern what fits. Both can be iterated independently.

7. Lunar Resource Utilization

In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) can reduce Starship delivery costs on a handful of categories, but the savings are smaller than often assumed at Phase 1b scale.

Component ISRU feasible? Savings potential
Track berms, radiation shielding Yes, immediately Replace 200-400 t of launched mass. At $2k/kg: $400-800M saved vs equivalent Earth-sourced fill
Structural sintered regolith blocks Yes, near-term 10-20 t of track support structures. Saves ~$20-40M
Solar panel substrates 2040s+ Potentially $40-80M if lunar silicon PV matures; high development cost offset
Liquid oxygen propellant for Starship return Yes, via electrolysis Reduces Starship cost per flight by 30-50%; multiplier on every kg delivered
REBCO superconductors No Requires rare-earth elements, precision ceramic coating, Earth manufacturing. Must be launched.
Electronics, power conversion No Must be launched. Not ISRU-replaceable at Phase 1b.

The most leveraged ISRU investment is enabling Starship to refuel with lunar oxygen, reducing the effective delivery cost from $2,000/kg toward $800-1,200/kg. That multiplies across every launched component and is worth pursuing in parallel with the track build.

9. Recommendation

Verdict: Ø2 m is the right design choice

Short-term (Phase 1b construction decision): build for Ø2 m from day one. The REBCO premium over Ø1 m is $76M (2.6% of total mid-case budget). The upgrade from Ø1 m to Ø2 m multiplies payload per shot by 4× and opens the packaged cargo market, which commands 5-20× higher revenue per tonne than bulk regolith. This premium pays back in hours of operation at market rates, not years.

Long-term (Phase 3 upgrade): Ø3 m makes sense when the Phase 3 track reaches 30 km (4.2 km/s, beyond lunar escape) and the economy justifies moving large assembled units. At that stage the system redesign is substantial regardless of the initial bore choice, so building Ø3 m now provides minimal Phase 3 leverage. Design Ø2 m with structural margins that permit a future Ø3 m upgrade sleeve.

Do not build Ø1 m unless budget is severely constrained below $2.0B. The $140M savings vs Ø2 m are real but the market ceiling is severe. Bulk water ice and regolith are a commodity; packaged cargo and propellant modules are a premium product. The system that gets built first will define the cislunar logistics market for decades. Build it with headroom.

The cost argument in one sentence

At $2.9B (mid), a Ø2 m SELENE launches bulk mass to cislunar orbit at $4/kg amortized vs Starship's $200-2,000/kg. The bore diameter changes this by 12% at most. Build the right bore for the market, not the cheapest bore for the budget.

10. Starship Delivery Strategy

A Phase 1b build requires approximately 119 tonnes of payload to the lunar surface (fixed mass only, before bore-specific components). At one Starship lunar lander mission per 100 t useful payload:

Mission sequence Mass (t) Starship flights Est. cost (@$2k/kg)
Solar arrays + power storage 73.5 1 $147M
Cryogenic plant + electronics 25 1 $50M
Construction robots + tools 20 1 $40M
REBCO + pods (Ø2 m) 4.9 + ~2 <1 (co-manifested) $14M
Total delivery (Ø2 m) ~125.4 t ~2-3 missions $251M

REBCO is extremely light for its value density (4.9 t for Ø2 m, at $215M in tape cost). It co-manifests easily on any mission and requires no special handling beyond temperature stability during transit. It is not the logistical challenge. The challenge is the cryogenic plant and the construction robots.
